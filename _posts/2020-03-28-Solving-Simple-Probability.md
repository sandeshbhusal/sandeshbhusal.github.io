#### Given A question:
> You have a Random Number generator. It can generate numbers with equal probabilities in the Range $$ (0, M) $$ where $$ M $$ is the input to the number generator. You are given two numbers as inputs to the machine, given as $$ A $$ and $$ B $$. There is a third constant number $$ C $$ that you need to watch out for. For the given pair of $$ A $$ and $$ B $$ what is the probability that any pair of number generated by the machine will have sum less than $$ C $$ ?
Bear with me for a moment. This will all be clear.
<br /><br />

##### 1. Explanation of the problem:
Given machine / generator is a uniform number distribution. For those of you who do not yet know what a uniform distribution is, it is a random number distribution where each each item has an equal probability of getting selected. Let us consider a bag with red, blue, black and green balls, each $$ 1 $$ in number. Then the distribution of balls is said to be uniform, because no matter what, each ball has a $$ 25% $$ probability of being selected from the bag. Back to the question. When we feed an input $$ A $$ to the machine, it can produce any number between $$ 0 $$  to $$ A $$ with equal probabilites, and again same is the case with $$ B $$. Now that the machine has generated two numbers, what will be the probability that the sum of those two will be lesser than a given number  $$ C $$?

##### 2. Solution:
I have one word for you. Bruteforce. The maximum numbers that can be produced are $$ A $$ and $$ B $$ themselves. So why not bruteforce each possible number pair and generate the probabilities? I'll tell you why. Because the time required for this grows with the order of $$ O(n^{2}) $$. This is not feasible for a solution and is an embarassment. So we need to optimize this problem.

Another way that this problem might be solved -- Using the two-pointer approach (or rather two-sum) that many of my fellow competitive programmers will already know about. For those of you who do not yet know about it, here is a simple explanation. 

<details>
    <summary> Two Sum using Two Pointers -- A shitty explanation </summary>
    Consider an array of numbers given as $$ X = \{ x_1, x_2, x_3, ... x_n \} $$. The size of the array is $$ N $$ and just for the sake of brevity, let us assume for a moment that the array is already sorted in the ascending order. Now, the challenge is to find the number pair whose sum is exactly equal to a given number $$ R $$. Well, let us analyse this problem. Certainly, if the sum of the last two numbers in the array is not greater or equal to the requirement then this problem is not solvable, i.e 

    $$ x_n + x_{n-1} = R $$

    or

    $$ x_n + x_{n-1} > R $$

    If the first condition is satisfied, then there's not much we need to do. But life's not that way now is it? In order to compute the pairs whose sum is exactly equal to $$ R $$, we make two pointers. One at the end of the array and one at the start. We increment the start pointer and decrement the end pointer, until we find a pair whose sum is exactly equal to the required sum. For more on this, refer to [this tutorial](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/two-pointers-technique).

</details>
<br /> 
Note a distinction. In the two-sum problem, the elements in the array are random and can be randomly distributed. However, in this problem, we have a uniform distribution of numbers! This makes our job a lot more easier. Now, given a required number $$ R $$ and bounds of generator $$ A $$ and $$ B $$, we can find the first pair that satisifies the sum equal to $$ R $$ as:

$$ Pair = [ max (A, B), R - max(A, B) ] $$

That's it! This is our first pair whose sum is exactly equal to $$ R $$. Now let us analyze this pair. It falls on a straight line that is the intercept between two axes at $$ (0, R) $$ and $$ (R, 0) $$. If we draw a straight line through these intercepts, the pair falls exactly on this line. What does this mean? Well, it means that every point in the straight line will have the sum of coordinates equal to $$ R $$. For every point between this line and the origin, the sum of coordinates is lesser than $$ R $$ and for each point above this line, the sum of the coordinates is greater than $$ R $$.

The problem is pretty much solved now. Given a number $$ A $$ and a number $$ B $$, the total number of possible pairs in the region will be 

$$ n(\{ (0, 0), (0, 1), ... (1, 0), (1, 1), ... (1, A), ... ... (A, B)\}) = A \times B $$

If we plot all these numbers, we can get a nice looking rectangle as follows:

All that is needed to be done now is superimposition of the two figures; the rectangle and the triangle on each other. The region of intersection between the triangle and the rectangle gives us our probability of the pair sum being lesser than $$ R  $$ and the remaining region denotes the probability of the pairs being greater than $$ R $$.

The probability is given by the area of the triangle, divided by the area of the rectangle. BUT Here's a corner case! What if the required number is very very large so that it forms a very big triangle! Then the division of area gives us a probability greater than 1, which is not possible. So we cap the max. probability to 1. Another case can be when the triangle and rectangle intersect -- in such a case the triangle will be inside the rectangle but not all portions. Then we can see a trapezoidal intersection. This case arises, when $$ min( A, B ) < R < max( A, B ) $$. In such a case, the probability is given by the area of the trapezoid. In such a case, the value of the intersection point is given by $$ max( A, B ) - ( min( A, B) + R ) $$. It may lie on either axis, depending on the choice of the axis for representation of $$ A $$ and $$ B $$. So the probabilities possible are:

$$ P =  \frac{ \frac{1}{2} \times R ^ 2 } { A \times B }  ... (1)$$

When the triangle lies entirely inside the rectangle.

$$ P = 1 ... (2) $$

When the rectangle lies entirely inside the triangle.

$$ P = \frac{ \frac {1}{2} ( B + R + R ) \times B } { A \times B} ... (3) $$

When the triangle and rectangle intersect, i.e. $$ min(A,B) < R < max(A, B) $$. This is the solution I came upon. If anything can be further improved, feel free to comment through [email](mailto:073bct539@ioe.edu.np).
